The Artist

Father Jerome Tupa

Father Jerome Tupa is a Benedictine monk of Saint John's Abbey in Collegeville, Minnesota — and one of the rare painters whose canvases hang comfortably in both monastery corridors and contemporary galleries. Ordained a priest and trained as a scholar of French literature, Tupa came to painting neither early nor casually: it arrived as a vocation within a vocation, a second calling pursued with monastic discipline and an utterly unmonastic palette.

His work is often described as fauvist — and the kinship with Matisse and Derain is real — but Tupa's color is not borrowed. It is devotional. Reds burn like sanctuary lamps; yellows arrive with the certainty of morning office; blues and teals hold the cool quiet of the abbey church at vespers.

The subject to which he returns, decade after decade, is pilgrimage. Tupa has painted his way along the Camino de Santiago, through the hill towns of Umbria, and across the sacred geographies of India and the American Midwest. The paintings that result are not travelogues; they are testimonies — records of what it feels like to move through the world attentively, expectantly, on foot and in faith.
